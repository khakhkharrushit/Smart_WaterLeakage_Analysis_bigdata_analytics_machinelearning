## Abstract

Urban water distribution systems are increasingly adopting smart metering technologies, generating massive volumes of high-velocity time-series data. However, a significant portion of treated water—often exceeding 30% in developing nations—is lost as Non-Revenue Water (NRW) due to undetected physical leakages and metering inaccuracies. Traditional monitoring approaches, which rely heavily on manual billing cycles or static threshold alerts, are insufficient for modern grids. They fail to detect small, creeping leaks that evolve slowly and often generate high rates of false positives during legitimate usage spikes, such as weekends or holidays. This project addresses these inefficiencies by proposing a robust, scalable Big Data Analytics framework designed to analyze consumption patterns, detect anomalies in real-time, and predict infrastructure failures.

Leveraging the distributed computing power of Apache Spark (PySpark), the proposed system overcomes the computational limitations of standalone servers. The methodology employs a hybrid analysis engine: first, Spark SQL is utilized to perform rapid temporal aggregations, constructing detailed historical consumption profiles. Second, a Statistical Anomaly Detection module computes dynamic baselines using moving averages and standard deviations, automatically adapting to seasonal variations in water demand. Third, a Rule-Based Leakage Identification layer filters these statistical outliers against known behavioral logic to mitigate false alarms. Finally, a **Machine Learning Predictive Engine** (Random Forest Classifier) is integrated to analyze complex behavioral metrics like usage volatility and night-time flow, creating a robust Early-Warning System (EWS).

The system is designed to process high-frequency data streams, ensuring that utility managers receive timely, actionable insights. By visualizing these patterns on an interactive, mathematical methodology-backed dashboard—complete with real-time "What-If" behavioral simulations—the project aims to minimize water wastage, optimize distribution pressure, and significantly improve revenue recovery for water boards.

## 1. Introduction

Water scarcity is a growing global crisis, exacerbated by rapid urbanization and aging infrastructure. In many metropolitan areas, Non-Revenue Water (NRW)—water that is produced but lost before it reaches the customer due to leaks or metering inaccuracies—accounts for a significant percentage of total supply. Addressing this requires a shift from reactive repairs to proactive, data-driven monitoring.

The project titled "Water Consumption Pattern Analysis and Leakage Detection Using Big Data Analytics" is justified by the necessity to handle the Volume and Velocity of data generated by modern smart water grids. A single city with 100,000 smart meters reporting data every 15 minutes generates millions of records daily. Traditional relational databases and standalone scripts cannot process this load efficiently. This project leverages Big Data technologies—specifically Apache Spark—to analyze these massive datasets. By focusing on Pattern Analysis and Predictive Machine Learning, the system moves beyond simple overflow alarms to understand how water is used, enabling detection of subtle, creeping leaks that standard methods miss.

### 1.1 Motivation
The primary motivation for this work is the economic and environmental cost of undetected leaks. Current systems often rely on manual reporting or expensive hardware sensors at every junction, which is not cost-effective for developing regions. There is a critical need for a software-centric solution that can analyze existing meter data to pinpoint faults. Furthermore, existing research often isolates leak detection from consumption profiling, leading to high false alarm rates during holidays or peak usage times. This project seeks to bridge that gap by combining statistical baselining with predictive AI logic.

### 1.1.1 Problem Statement
Most existing water management systems lack the computational capacity to analyze historical time-series data in real-time. They struggle to differentiate between a legitimate sudden increase in water usage (e.g., filling a tank) and an actual pipe burst. This results in alert fatigue for operators and continued water loss.

### 6.8 Predictive Machine Learning (Early-Warning System)
In addition to hard-coded heuristics, a proactive **Random Forest Classifier** was integrated to act as an Early-Warning System (EWS). 

**Resolving Target Leakage & Behavioral Extraction:**
During initial model iterations, utilizing direct sensory leak flags resulted in "target leakage" predicting leaks based on the leak flag itself. To create a true early-warning system, the ML feature space was intentionally restricted to **purely behavioral indicators**:
* `std_usage` (Usage Volatility: high standard deviation indicates erratic micro-bursts).
* `night_avg_usage` (Usage strictly bracketed between 12:00 AM - 05:00 AM; a prime indicator of continuous trickle leaks like broken cisterns).
* `max_spike_ratio` and `avg_usage`.

By forcing the Random Forest to learn strictly from behavioral water patterns, the AI can proactively assign a "High Risk Probability" to a household *before* physical sensors fail to a critical state.

## 7. Results and Analysis

The system was evaluated on a dataset of 4,320,000 records from 1,000 unique smart meter households. The analysis revealed the following key insights:

* Peak water consumption occurs during morning and evening hours, consistent with residential usage patterns.
* A total of 247,960 leak events were detected via dynamic statistical heuristics, representing approximately 5.7% of all records.
* **Machine Learning Robustness:** The Predictive Random Forest model successfully learned localized behavioral trends. By applying 5-Fold Cross-Validation, the model achieved high ROC-AUC accuracy based strictly on night usage and volume volatility, proving leaks can be probabilistically detected without relying on mechanical sensors alone.
* 486 households were classified as high-risk, with 232 categorized as critical requiring immediate physical inspection.
* The interactive GUI allows municipal engineers to explore 100% of the dataset with zero coding required, bridging the gap between raw Big Data and operational management.
